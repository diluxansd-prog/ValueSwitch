If you've been on the same mobile network for three years or more, you're almost certainly overpaying. Loyalty pricing — where existing customers pay 20-40% more than new ones for the same plan — is the dirty secret of UK telecom, and Ofcom has known about it since 2018 but never properly cracked down.

The fix is simple: switch. The fix is also annoying because mobile networks don't want you to leave, so they've added friction at every step. Here's the **actual UK switching process in May 2026**, including the bits the providers don't tell you.

## The 30-second summary

1. **Text PAC to 65075** to get your transfer code (7 digits, valid 30 days)
2. **Sign up with your new network** and give them the PAC code
3. **Wait 1 working day** — your number transfers automatically
4. **Old contract auto-cancels** the moment the number ports (no separate cancellation needed)

If you do this on a Tuesday morning, your number is on the new network by Wednesday afternoon. Total downtime: usually under 4 hours, sometimes zero.

That's the legal Ofcom-mandated process. Now here's where networks get sneaky.

## The PAC code "security delay" trick (and how to bypass it)

Three networks — **Vodafone**, **O2**, and **EE** — have started adding a **24-72 hour "security review"** before they release your PAC code. They claim this is for fraud prevention. It's actually friction designed to make you cancel the switch.

Ofcom rules say PAC codes must be issued **within 60 seconds** of a valid request. If you're delayed, you're entitled to escalate.

**What to do if your PAC is delayed past 60 seconds:**

1. Reply STOP to the original request, wait 5 minutes, text PAC to 65075 again
2. If still no response after 10 minutes, call your network's customer service and quote: *"This is an Ofcom-mandated 60-second SLA. I want my PAC issued now."*
3. If they still refuse, file an Ofcom complaint at **ofcom.org.uk/complaints/mobile** — they take this very seriously

90% of "security delays" disappear the moment you mention Ofcom by name.

## STAC vs PAC — pick the right one

There are two transfer codes and they do different things:

- **PAC** (text PAC to 65075): Keep your number, port to the new network
- **STAC** (text STAC to 75075): Cancel the contract entirely, get a new number on the new network

Almost everyone wants PAC. STAC is only useful if:

- You're escaping a stalker / abusive ex who has your number
- You're getting a brand-new business line and don't want callers from your personal life
- Your old number has been compromised in a breach

If in doubt, request PAC. You can always abandon a PAC code (it expires in 30 days) but you can't recover a number once you've STAC'd it.

## The early-termination trap

If your contract isn't finished, the mobile network can charge you the **remaining months × your monthly cost**, minus VAT (or "ETF" — early termination fee). For a 24-month contract with 8 months left at £45/mo, that's roughly £300.

**Three legitimate ways out without paying ETF:**

1. **Mid-contract price rises >2%** — Ofcom rule from January 2025 says any "unexpected" price rise above CPI gives you a free 30-day cancellation window. Most networks raised by ~7.9% in April 2025; that triggered the right to leave for free.
2. **Contract on a "rolling 30-day" basis** — Vodafone, O2 and Three all offer this for ~£3-5/mo extra; ETF doesn't apply.
3. **Death/serious illness/bankruptcy** — networks must waive fees with proof. Be aware: a death certificate or DWP letter is required, not just your word.

If none of these apply, sometimes the cheapest move is to **wait it out**. An ETF of £200 with 8 months left = £25/mo. If your new network saves you £15/mo, you're paying £10/mo extra for early flexibility — usually not worth it.

## How to actually compare deals (the bit nobody teaches you)

Most comparison sites show you the **headline monthly cost** and that's it. The real cost is:

`Total monthly cost × contract length + setup fee + handset upgrade fee - cashback - first-month discount`

For a typical 24-month contract, the difference between two "£20/mo" deals can be £80 once you factor in setup, cashback and first-month deals.

## The "double-billing" scam to watch for

Roughly 1 in 8 UK switches results in **double-billing for one month** — you get charged by your old network for the period your number was already on the new one.

This is illegal under the Ofcom 2024 "Switch It On" reforms but it still happens because billing systems are slow.

**What to do**:

1. Take screenshots of your final bill from the old network the day after your number ports
2. If you get charged for any period after the port date, contact the old network with the screenshots and ask for a refund
3. If they refuse, file a complaint at **resolver.co.uk** — they have a free template for "telecoms double-billing"

90% of cases are refunded within 14 days once Resolver's involved. The other 10% go to Ofcom.

## What about pay-as-you-go?

Switching from PAYG to a contract (or vice versa) follows the exact same PAC/STAC process. There's no contract to break on PAYG. The only difference: you usually want to **use up your remaining PAYG credit before switching**, because most networks don't refund unused balance.

Three exceptions: **Lebara** and **giffgaff** both refund unused PAYG credit on request (just contact support before switching). **Vodafone PAYG** refunds via cheque only — they'll send it 4-6 weeks after you ask, slow but legitimate.
