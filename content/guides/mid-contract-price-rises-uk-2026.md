In January 2025, Ofcom did something rare: they banned a specific business practice. UK mobile and broadband providers could no longer write contracts that raised your bill by **CPI + 3.9% every April** — a clause that had quietly added £40-£90 to the average annual bill since 2019.

Sixteen months later, in April 2026, the four biggest UK networks all raised prices anyway. Here's exactly how, by how much, and what your rights are if your bill went up without warning.

## What Ofcom actually banned (and what they didn't)

The January 2025 rules say two specific things:

1. **No more variable mid-contract increases**. You can't sign a contract for "£25 + CPI + 3.9% per year" because the actual end price is unknowable.
2. **All in-contract increases must be quoted in £ at sign-up**. So a contract can say "£25/mo for year 1, £27/mo for year 2" but the £27 has to be in the contract paperwork upfront.

What Ofcom did NOT ban:
- Fixed-£ increases (e.g. "+£3 in April every year")
- One-off "service review" price changes
- Price changes between contracts (i.e. when you renew at end-of-contract)

**The result**: networks moved from variable percentage increases to fixed-£ increases — but the amounts are similar to what you'd have got under the old system.

## What each major UK network did in April 2026

### EE — bumped £3.50/mo on most plans

EE's standard pay-monthly tariffs all went up by £3.50/mo on 1 April 2026. The change was disclosed in contracts signed from October 2024 onwards — so legally they're in the clear, but most customers didn't notice the small print.

**Annual impact for an average customer**: £42 extra per year.

### O2 — bumped £3/mo, with a 30-day cancellation window for older contracts

O2 raised prices by £3/mo on contracts signed before October 2024 — and because those contracts had the old "CPI+3.9%" clause that's now unenforceable, O2 was forced to give those customers a **30-day right to leave penalty-free**.

If you signed an O2 contract before October 2024 and got a price rise notice in March 2026, **you can still leave free of charge** until 30 days after the rise. Most customers haven't taken this up because the notice was buried in account emails.

### Vodafone — bumped £2.50/mo on standard tariffs, nothing on Vodafone Pro

Vodafone split their customer base. Standard tariff customers got a £2.50/mo bump in April 2026. Vodafone Pro customers (the premium tier) saw no increase — Vodafone is using price stability as a Pro selling point now.

### Three — bumped £2/mo plus rebrand

Three completed its merger with Vodafone in 2025 (now operating jointly under the VodafoneThree brand for some purposes). Three customers saw £2/mo increases in April 2026 plus tariff "simplification" that pushed many onto more expensive plans.

### Talkmobile — held flat

Talkmobile (Vodafone-owned, see our [Vodafone vs Talkmobile comparison](/guides/mobile/vodafone-vs-talkmobile-2026)) held headline prices completely flat in April 2026. New customers signed in April 2026 are on the same headline tariffs as customers from October 2024.

### Lebara — held flat, expanded rollover

Lebara not only held prices flat, they expanded their data rollover from 30 days to 60 days. Heavy-then-light users benefit most.

### giffgaff — held flat

giffgaff has held the same headline prices for 14 consecutive months. Their goodybag pricing has been the same since February 2025.

### Sky Mobile — bumped £1.50/mo

Sky's mobile customers saw the smallest increase: £1.50/mo, plus an extra 5GB of data on most tariffs as a goodwill gesture. Effective price-per-GB actually fell slightly.

## The 30-day cancellation right — does it apply to you?

The Ofcom 2025 reforms gave customers two specific rights:

1. **If your contract was signed before October 2024 with a CPI+X clause** and you got a price rise in 2025 or 2026, you have a **30-day right to leave penalty-free** from the date the rise was announced.
2. **If your contract is post-October 2024 with a fixed-£ clause** disclosed at sign-up, you do **NOT** have a right to leave — the increase was contractually agreed.

To check your contract:
1. Log into your network's app or web account
2. Look for "Contract terms" or "Service agreement"
3. Search for the words "annual price change" or "April"
4. If it says "CPI" anywhere, you probably have the right to leave free

If you find a CPI clause and you're within 30 days of a price rise email, this is **your free escape window**. Switch now — see our [how to switch networks guide](/guides/mobile/how-to-switch-mobile-network-uk-2026) for the actual process.

## How to lock in current prices for 2 years

Networks that disclosed fixed-£ increases at sign-up are actually predictable. If you sign a 24-month contract with EE today (May 2026), they're legally required to spell out the April 2027 and April 2028 prices in the contract.

This is **the closest thing to a price guarantee** the UK has had in years. Look for:

- "Year 1 price: £20"
- "Year 2 price (from April 2027): £23"
- "Total cost over 24 months: £528"

Three is currently the best at this — their post-October-2024 contracts disclose all 24 months' pricing clearly. EE and O2's contracts are technically compliant but harder to read.

## Networks that promise no in-contract increases

A small number of UK MVNOs promise **zero in-contract price changes for the contract length**:

- **Talkmobile**: Fixed price for 12-month contracts
- **giffgaff**: No-contract goodybags only — price changes affect new customers, not your current goodybag
- **Lebara**: Fixed price for 30-day and 12-month plans
- **1pMobile**: Fixed price for 12-month plans

If you genuinely want to lock in a price, these are the networks. The trade-off is they're all SIM-only — none of them sell handset contracts.
