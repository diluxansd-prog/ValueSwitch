UK full-fibre coverage hit 78% of premises in early 2026, according to Ofcom's latest connected nations report. That headline hides a lot. "Available in your postcode" doesn't always mean "installable at your front door" — the cabinet, the duct, the riser, and your specific line all need to be ready, and one stuck step kills the install.

This guide walks through the actual postcode-check process for May 2026: the four checkers worth using, how to interpret each one, and what to do if your answer is "almost".

## The 4 postcode checkers worth using

Each of these checks a different part of the network. You probably need to run at least 3 to get the truth.

### 1. Ofcom Connected Nations Checker

**URL**: checker.ofcom.org.uk/en-gb/broadband-coverage

**What it tells you**: Which fibre providers technically have a presence at your postcode. Ofcom aggregates data from all UK ISPs.

**What it doesn't tell you**: Whether the install is actually plug-and-play, or whether you need a £200 engineer visit and a 4-week wait.

**Use it for**: A first-pass sanity check. If Ofcom says no full-fibre providers serve your postcode, the others won't either.

### 2. Openreach Checker

**URL**: openreach.com/fibre-broadband (postcode tool, not the BT consumer site)

**What it tells you**: Whether **Openreach's** fibre network reaches your address. Openreach is the wholesale infrastructure used by BT, Sky, TalkTalk, EE, Plusnet, Vodafone and most other major providers.

**What it doesn't tell you**: Whether the alternative networks (Be Fibre, Hyperoptic, CityFibre, Community Fibre) reach you. These run their own networks separately from Openreach.

**Use it for**: Confirming if mainstream providers (BT, Sky, Vodafone, etc.) can actually connect you.

### 3. Be Fibre / CityFibre / Hyperoptic checkers

Each "altnet" (alternative network) has its own postcode checker. The big ones in May 2026:

- **Be Fibre**: bemoreconnected.co.uk/check
- **CityFibre**: cityfibre.com/residential
- **Hyperoptic**: hyperoptic.com/check
- **Community Fibre** (London only): communityfibre.co.uk
- **Gigaclear** (rural focus): gigaclear.com/check

**What they tell you**: Whether their specific network reaches your address. These networks are usually faster (1Gbps standard, 8Gbps available in some areas) and cheaper per Mbps than Openreach-based providers.

**What they don't tell you**: Whether you can install today. Many list addresses as "available" 6-12 months before the actual install crew arrives.

**Use it for**: Finding out if you've got access to a faster/cheaper altnet alongside the mainstream options.

### 4. The honest one: thinkbroadband.com

**URL**: thinkbroadband.com/broadband (address search)

**What it tells you**: Real-world test results from people at your postcode — actual achieved speeds, install difficulties reported, providers used.

**What it doesn't tell you**: It's user-generated, so a postcode with few users has no data.

**Use it for**: Sanity-checking the providers' marketing claims. If Be Fibre says "1000 Mbps" but thinkbroadband shows real users averaging 320 Mbps in your postcode, that's the honest number.

## What "available" actually means at each checker

The same word means different things across checkers:

| Checker says | Reality |
|--------------|---------|
| Ofcom: "Available" | Some provider lists this postcode in their coverage data |
| Openreach: "Live" | Active fibre to your cabinet; standard install (~2-3 weeks lead time) |
| Openreach: "Build complete" | Engineering finished; install bookings open |
| Openreach: "Ready for service" | Same as live but newer term post-March 2026 |
| Altnet: "Available" | They've trenched the fibre to your street |
| Altnet: "Available — pre-order" | Cable on the street but not connected to your house yet |
| Altnet: "Coming soon" | Marketing speak for 6-12 months out |

If you see "pre-order" or "coming soon" anywhere, the provider is asking you to commit to their service before they've actually built the install. Some honour the timeline; many don't. Read the cancellation terms before pre-ordering.

## The hidden install steps that block 1 in 8 fibre orders

Even when every checker says yes, fibre installs hit physical reality. Common reasons orders get cancelled at engineer visit:

1. **No duct from cabinet to property**. The fibre exists in the road but never got trenched to your front door. Engineer can't lay new duct without council approval.
2. **Existing duct collapsed**. Old copper duct from BT cabinet has filled with mud over decades. Re-cabling needs civils work (£300-£800).
3. **Master socket location wrong**. If your fibre socket needs to go in a specific room and it's on the wrong floor, install can be deferred until building work resolves it.
4. **Landlord permission missing**. Renters need landlord written consent for any drilling. Engineers can't proceed without it.
5. **Communal block riser locked**. Flats need building manager access on the day. Common cause of failed installs in cities.

## What to do when checkers disagree

Common scenario: Ofcom says "yes", Openreach says "yes", Be Fibre says "available", but Sky's order page says "no service". This happens a lot.

The reason is usually that Sky checks **at the address level** (line ID), not just the postcode. Their internal database shows your specific address can't yet take the order — even if the postcode checker said yes.

**The fix**: Don't trust postcode-level checks alone. Always run an order through a provider's own address-level check before signing. If the provider says yes at address level, you're 95% likely to actually get connected.

## How to choose between Openreach providers and altnets

Once you know which networks reach you, choose by:

### Pick Openreach (BT, Sky, Plusnet, EE, Vodafone, TalkTalk) if:
- You want the lowest possible monthly cost (volume keeps prices low)
- You want bundled options (TV + mobile + broadband)
- You're in a rural area where altnets don't reach
- You value brand familiarity / store-based support

### Pick an altnet (Be Fibre, Hyperoptic, CityFibre, Gigaclear) if:
- You want the fastest speeds at the lowest price-per-Mbps
- You're a heavy user (lots of upload, gaming, work-from-home)
- You're in a city or new-build estate where they have full coverage
- You're happy with online-only support

In London and Manchester especially, **Be Fibre and CityFibre's gigabit packages are routinely cheaper than BT's 100 Mbps tier** — a 10x speed advantage for less money. The catch: their support is purely online, and outage-handling can be slower than BT's.

## The Ofcom "address level" rule that protects you

Since April 2024, Ofcom requires UK ISPs to check **at the address level**, not just the postcode level, before accepting an order. If they take your order and then can't deliver:

- **Within 14 days**: You can cancel for free. ISP must refund any fees paid.
- **Within 30 days**: You can cancel for free. ISP may charge for installation work already done (but not for the contract itself).
- **After 30 days**: ISP must offer either a working alternative service at the same price, or release you from the contract penalty-free.

If your install gets repeatedly delayed past these windows and the ISP refuses to release you, file an Ofcom complaint at ofcom.org.uk/complaints. Most are resolved in 2-3 weeks.

## Bottom line — the quick version

1. Run **Ofcom's checker** first — sanity check
2. Run **Openreach's checker** — confirms BT/Sky/Vodafone availability
3. Run **2-3 altnet checkers** for your postcode — Be Fibre, CityFibre, Hyperoptic
4. Check **thinkbroadband.com** for real-world speeds at your address
5. Always do an **address-level check** through the actual provider before signing

If 3+ checkers agree, you're good. If 1-2 disagree, run an address check on the disputed provider before committing — they're usually right.
